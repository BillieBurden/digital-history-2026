The dataset was compiled by the Washington Post and then published on GitHub through one of their investigative data reporters, Andrew Ba Tran. The data comes from the U.S. Department of the Interior’s (DOI) Federal Indian Boarding School Initiative Investigative Report Vol. II and the Washington Post’s year-long investigation of the boarding schools. The DOI’s research goals were to investigate “the loss of human life and lasting consequences of the Federal Indian boarding school system” and to “assist in the effort to recover the histories of these institutions” (Newland 9). Whereas the Post created this file and others to “facilitate additional research” (Hedgpeth).

Each row is a record for one Indian boarding school. The columns then list the details for that school: its name and state, its years of operation, the specific Tribes that attended, and any other names the school used. There are also counts for the deceased people and burial sites found by both the DOI and the Washington Post, with notes on whether those graves were onsite (at the school) or off-site (in another cemetery). Finally, each row has the school's exact latitude and longitude coordinates and a note on whether the location was verified.

There are many limitations to this dataset and the overall research. The DOI report acknowledges that its count of 973 deaths is incomplete because many federal records were destroyed, lost, or never created. More confinements come from the date range of their research. Victims who do not fall between the years of 1819-1969 could get omitted from the data, ultimately narrowing the qualifications for who gets counted and who doesn’t. Additionally, The Washington Post noted that school administrators, such as Richard Henry Pratt at Carlisle, often sent terminally ill children home so their deaths would occur off-campus and not appear on official school registers (Hedgpeth). With a broader view that could show that the perpetrators controlled the validity of the data, leaving children unaccounted for. Another issue arises due to the DOI withholding the names of the children they identified, making it impossible to verify how much their list overlaps with the Post’s larger count. This creates a gap in our ability to cross-reference specific identities. Additionally, the dataset relies on a Western geospatial grid that cannot easily account for lost cemeteries/burial sites. For example, the Post used historical maps to find burial sites that have since been paved over or turned into farmland, such as the soybean field at the Genoa Indian Industrial School.

UPDATE for Milestone #3

Producing the code for this project felt similar to Milestone #2, but I found it to be easier to know where I wanted my next steps because I was working with my own argument. The geographic side of my hypothesis aligned with what came up in the visualizations, but my time-related side had some surprises. When I saw the smaller but more consistent spike in the line chart I did some research for why and realized I had completely forgotten about the Dawes Act of 1887.  I had not considered it when thinking of my data because my preliminary research focused mainly on the start and end of the Indian Boarding Schools. The biggest surprise was the spike around 1935 even after the Indian Reorganization Act had been enacted in 1934. Brenda Child who is a professor at the University of Minnesota described why there is an increase in enrollment and activity with these boarding schools “largely at the request of American Indian families who used them as a form of poverty relief during the Great Depression so their families could survive” (Child). So my initial hypothesis was both correct and incorrect in some areas and made me realize how difficult it can be to consider all historical events and how they aren't always obvious.
	Some of my choices could have also influenced the end product. There were 17 schools that had multiple sets of dates that were separated by a pipe. I took the first date to keep the data of the initial expansions consistent, but there could be interesting findings within those date ranges and why there is more than one. I also took out 7 schools that had missing dates, 6 of those were from Arizona and the other from New Mexico. However, I do not think much was lost with this choice because it is only 7 out of 418 and both of those states had the highest counts anyway. 
	The main limitation of the line chart is that it only captures schools that opened in a given year, not how many were actively operating at any one time. The sharp drop after 1935 can look like the system collapsed, but what it really reflects is that new construction stopped. Many schools continued operating well into the following decades, which is worth keeping in mind when reading the visualization.
